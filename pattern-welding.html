Pattern Welding

History · Steel Selection · Technique Taxonomy · Etching · Simulation Design
01 / 06 Historical context
Origins

Pattern welding emerged in Northern Europe from approximately 200 AD to 1000 AD, spanning the Migration Period through the Viking Age. Celtic, Germanic, and Norse smiths combined inconsistent iron blooms of varying carbon content to produce more reliable blades. The visible pattern was a byproduct of structural engineering.

Pattern welding is not Wootz. Wootz (crucible Damascus) is a completely different metallurgical process — a single high-carbon steel ingot melted in a sealed crucible, producing carbide banding during slow cooling. Wootz originated in India circa 300 BC. The term "Damascus steel" conflates both traditions; serious bladesmiths prefer "pattern-welded steel."

The modern revival began in the 1970s-80s with Bill Moran (who reintroduced Damascus to the American Bladesmith Society), Jim Hrisoulas, and the "Montana Mafia" group. Devin Thomas popularized the 1084/15N20 combination in the mid-1990s, establishing the modern standard.

02 / 06 Material science
Steel Selection

Manganese (not carbon) is the primary driver of dark etching. Carbon homogenizes between layers during forging — any contrast is from alloying elements. Nickel resists acid and stays bright.

1084
Dark Layer · Standard

Carbon 0.84%, Manganese 0.75%. Elevated Mn causes dark etching. The modern standard dark-layer steel. Welds at 2300-2350°F. No chromium (which would inhibit flux bonding).

15N20
Bright Layer · Standard

Carbon 0.75%, Nickel 2.0%. Nickel forms a passive oxide layer that resists FeCl₃ acid attack — stays bright and proud after etching. Welds at the same temperature range as 1084.

W2
Dark Layer · Alternative

Carbon 0.95-1.10%, trace tungsten. Water-hardening. Excellent hamon potential when differentially hardened. Beautiful dark etch but less contrast than 1084.

L6
Bright Layer · Alternative

Carbon 0.65-0.75%, Nickel 1.5%. Lower nickel content than 15N20 gives reduced contrast but excellent toughness. Historically used in band saw blades.

1095
Dark Layer · Alternative

Carbon 0.95%, Manganese 0.4%. Common but lower Mn than 1084 gives less etch contrast. Very hard, takes an excellent edge.

O1
Dark Layer · Specialty

Carbon 0.85-1.00%, Manganese 1.2%. Very dark etch from high Mn. Oil-hardening. Risk of delayed cracking — not forgiving. For experienced smiths.

Layer Count & Folding

Beyond ~500 layers, individual bands become thinner than the etching resolution (~5-10μm) and optically disappear. "So fine it looked like static on an old TV."

04 / 06 Technique taxonomy
Pattern Techniques
Random / Wild
OP-1: FOLD + OP-2: DRAW

Basic fold-and-forge with no deliberate manipulation. Organic, flowing, wood-grain-like lines. Each piece is unique. The default output of forging without intentional pattern control.

  • Simulation: Perlin domain-warp noise (current organic mode)
  • Controls: layer count, fold count, turbulence
Twist
OP-3: TWIST

Billet drawn to a square bar, heated, twisted along its axis using tongs/vise, then forged flat. Creates concentric spirals. Grinding deeper reveals the star center.

  • Measured in revolutions per inch (0.5 = bold, 2 = fine)
  • ~115-120 layers optimal. Round corners before twisting.
  • Two bars with opposing twist → chevron center line
  • Simulation: sinusoidal bands, parametric turns + grind depth
Ladder
OP-4: GROOVE

Grooves pressed or ground perpendicular to the layers. When forged flat, the interrupted layers create parallel horizontal "rungs." 300+ layers recommended for crisp lines.

  • Press method: 1/4" rods welded to die block
  • Grooves pressed to ~2× finished thickness
  • Stagger grooves between sides for better effect
  • Simulation: periodic Gaussian indentations
Feather / Herringbone
OP-5: CUT + REARRANGE

C-shaped curves pressed into billet, split vertically, one half flipped and rewelded. The seam becomes the central spine/vein of the feather with V-chevrons radiating outward.

  • Alternative: twist bar split + flip + reweld
  • 200-300 layers around the central vein
  • The most photographed Damascus pattern
  • Simulation: V-fold with bilateral symmetry
Raindrop
OP-4: GROOVE (radial)

Shallow dimples drilled or pressed into the billet (~1/3 thickness), then forged/ground flat. Each dimple punches through several layers, creating concentric rings when revealed.

  • 30-60 layers per side optimal (67-72 total)
  • Ball bearings in die blocks for uniform dimples
  • Deeper dimples = more concentric rings per drop
  • Simulation: circular Gaussian indentations
Heart
OP-5: DOME + SPLIT + MIRROR

Dome pressed/ground into billet face, ground flat to reveal concentric arcs, split through center of dome, one half flipped and rewelded. Mirrored arcs create heart shape at the seam.

  • The "dome-grind-split-reweld" technique
  • Split position determines heart proportions
  • V-notch in bottom creates the pointed base
  • Simulation: arched bilateral parabolas + V-base
Mosaic / Turkish
OP-6: PIXEL ASSEMBLY

Contrasting steel tiles arranged in a mild-steel canister as designed pixels. Forge-welded under press. Can be re-sliced, rotated, and restacked for pattern magnification. Stars, feathers, custom images possible.

  • Design on graph paper first — every pixel planned
  • 3D-printed PLA cores burn out during first heat
  • Critical temp: 1150-1250°C (below = no bond, above = brittle)
  • Simulation: discrete pixel grid cross-section
Cable Damascus
WIRE CONSOLIDATION

Carbon steel cable (Extra IPS, essentially 1075) forge-welded into solid steel. Pattern forms from the decarburization layer (~0.005-0.010") between every wire. Considered one of the most difficult forging jobs.

  • Avoid galvanized cable (zinc is toxic + inhibits welding)
  • Saturate with borax flux before welding
  • Quality depends on complete wire consolidation
  • Visual: rope-like twisted fiber bundles
05 / 06 Revealing the pattern
Ferric Chloride Etching

The blade must be hardened and tempered before etching. You will not get good contrast on annealed or normalized steel.

Surface prep — Sand to 400-600 grit. Do not buff. Clean with acetone.

Solution — 50/50 ferric chloride and distilled water. Never tap water. Distilled must be <1 year old.

Submersion — Suspend on wire, don't touch container bottom. Brush with soft toothbrush at 1-min intervals. ~10 minutes total.

Neutralize — Baking soda solution for 10 minutes. Spray with Windex (ammonia ensures complete neutralization).

Polish — 2000-grit on flats to brighten raised nickel layers while keeping etched valleys dark.

Coffee soak — Optional: strong instant coffee overnight for dramatically darker carbon-steel layers.

Repeat — 3 cycles of 10-min etch with oxide removal for best contrast.

06 / 06 Future: billet builder
Simulation Design · Phase 2

The current simulator generates patterns via mathematical functions that approximate each technique's output. Phase 2 will introduce a Billet Builder — a playful interface where the user constructs a virtual billet from scratch:

1. Draw — Sketch or select steel pieces (1084 dark, 15N20 bright) on a cross-section grid.
2. Stack — Arrange alternating layers. Choose starting layer count and steel ratio.
3. Operate — Apply forging operations in sequence: fold, draw, twist, groove, cut-and-rearrange.
4. Preview — See intermediate results after each operation. The billet state machine tracks layer geometry.
5. Reveal — Grind at a chosen depth to see the final pattern. Adjust grind depth with a slider.
6. Etch — Apply virtual FeCl₃ etching with contrast and depth controls.
7. Export — Send the revealed pattern to the blade renderer or export as SVG/PNG.

Architecture: the billet state is a 2D array of layer boundaries. Each operation transforms the array. The renderer computes which layer is visible at each pixel by checking which boundaries the grind depth falls between. This maps directly to the Operation Taxonomy documented in damascus-pattern-physics.md.
